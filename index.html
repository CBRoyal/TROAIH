The Interstate Highways around America serve as the transportation backbones of the U.S., offering millions of Americas the opportunity to drive on them for commutes, long-distance travel, freight transport daily, etc. Interstate highways have the highest speed limits in a given area, which are determined by specific states, and they improve the mobility of military troops to and from airports, seaports, rail terminals, and other military bases. They also connect to other roads of the Strategic Highway Network, which is a system of roads identified as critical to the U.S. Department of Defense. There are a couple types of Interstate Highways including primary, auxiliary, regional, and business interstate highways, and this webpage takes you on a road trip down all of the different types of Interstate highways.

Primary Interstate Highways

Primary Interstate highways, a.k.a. main-line Interstate Highways, serve as the Interstate Highway System's backbones. They're offered one-/ two-digit route numbers less than 100 with east-west Interstates being assigned with even numbers and north-south Interstates being assigned odd numbers. Lower numbered east-west Interstates run in the south, lower numbered north-south Interstates run in the west, higher numbered east-west Interstates run in the north, and higher numbered north-south Interstates run in the east. There are currently 71 primary Interstate highways in the U.S. and while they're those that are fully completed, they're also those that have segments still under development.

Auxiliary Interstate Highways

Auxiliary Interstate highways are subsets of the primary Interstate highways that fall into three categories: spur routes, bypasses, and beltways. Some of these Interstates connect to their parent primary Interstate at one end, but to another route at the other end. Auxiliary Interstate highways are assigned three-digit numbers in a range from 100-999 with their parent primary Interstate highway number being included at the end. Spur routes are assigned with an odd hundreds digits, while bypasses and beltways are assigned even hundreds digits. Unlike primary Interstates, auxilary Interstates are assigned either north-south or east-west, directionsdepending on the general orientation of the highway and not the route number. Some looped auxiliary Interstates will use inner-outer directions instead of compass directions to avoid ambiguity. These types of highways are repeated in different states along the mainline, but not within a state.

Regional Interstate Highways

The Interstate Highway System also extends out to Alaska, Hawaii, and Puerto Rico as as they're all part of the territories of the United States. There are 3 Interstate highways each in Hawaii and Puerto Rico, and 4 in Alaska, each assigned a certain number and prefix before it, depending on their territory. These certain types of Interstates aren't required to meet the same standards as the mainland Interstates, as highways in Alaska and Puerto Rico are designed with geometric and construction standards adequate for current and probable future traffic demands and the need for the locals of their specific region.

Business Interstate Highways

Some Interstate highways can also be business routes, which connect a central or commercial district of a city or town with an Interstate bypass. The main purpose of these routes are to serve a certain downtown area, as they're routed along surface roads. Since business Interstates are routed along surface roads, they don't have to meet the standards of other Interstate highways to qualify for an Interstate route by the Interstate Highway System. Like auxiliary Interstates, these types of Interstates can be repeated from state to state along their route, but only in several locations within the same state. Unlike the other types of Interstate highways, they use green sheilds instead of red and blue and are classified as either business loops or spurs. A business loop has both of its ends connected to its parent Interstate highway, while a business spur has one end at the parent Interstate highway, and the other at a specific destination, often in a city or town.

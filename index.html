Interstate

Highways

The Interstate Highways around America serve as the transportation backbones of the U.S., offering millions of Americas the opportunity to drive on them for commutes, long-distance travel, freight transport daily, etc. Interstate highways have the highest speed limits in a given area, which are determined by specific states, and they improve the mobility of military troops to and from airports, seaports, rail terminals, and other military bases. They also connect to other roads of the Strategic Highway Network, which is a system of roads identified as critical to the U.S. Department of Defense. There are a couple types of Interstate Highways including primary, auxiliary, regional, and business interstate highways, and this webpage takes you on a road trip down all of the different types of Interstate highways.

Primary Interstate Highways

Primary Interstate highways, a.k.a. main-line Interstate Highways, serve as the Interstate Highway System's backbones. They're offered one-/ two-digit route numbers less than 100 with east-west Interstates being assigned with even numbers and north-south Interstates being assigned odd numbers. Lower numbered east-west Interstates run in the south, lower numbered north-south Interstates run in the west, higher numbered east-west Interstates run in the north, and higher numbered north-south Interstates run in the east. There are currently 71 primary Interstate highways in the U.S. and while they're those that are fully completed, they're also those that have segments still under development.
